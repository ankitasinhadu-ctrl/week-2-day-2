In this lab, I started by writing the simplest possible instructions (called zero-shot prompts) for three tasks — figuring out if a customer is happy or unhappy, writing product descriptions, and pulling out key information from customer messages. When I ran each prompt 5, 10, and 15 times, I quickly noticed the AI gave a different style of answer almost every single time — for example, sometimes it said "Positive" and other times it said "The customer seems to be very positive about the product!", which sounds similar to us humans but breaks any code trying to read it automatically. The first fix I tried was simply being more specific in my instructions, like telling the AI "reply with exactly one word" or "always return your answer in JSON format" — this helped a lot and made the answers much more predictable. But the real game-changer was giving the AI actual examples of what a good answer looks like (called few-shot prompting), almost like showing a new employee a filled-in form before asking them to fill one out themselves. I also tried asking the AI to think through the problem step by step before answering (called Chain-of-Thought), which worked especially well for the data extraction task because it stopped the AI from rushing to an answer and missing important details. If I did this again, I would spend less time running the broken prompts over and over — the problems were obvious after just 5 runs — and use that saved time to test my improved prompts on more different types of customer messages, because a prompt that works perfectly on one message can still struggle when the wording changes slightly.

 Key Lessons Learned

Zero-shot prompts are not production-ready. They work occasionally but fail at scale.
Being specific always helps. Telling the AI exactly what format you want — even just saying "one word only" — can double your consistency score overnight.
Examples beat instructions. Showing the AI what a good answer looks like (few-shot) is almost always more effective than describing it in words.
Chain-of-Thought is powerful for complex tasks. Asking the AI to reason step by step before answering reduces careless mistakes significantly.
Always test with multiple runs. One successful test means nothing. You need at least 10-15 runs to understand how reliable a prompt really is.
Test on variations, not just your original example. A prompt that works on one input can still fail on slightly different wording.


What problem does this solve?

Most people who are new to AI assume that if a prompt works once, it will always work. This project proves that assumption wrong. When I ran the same basic prompt 15 times in a row, I got 15 different styles of answers — some short, some long, some formatted as JSON, some as plain sentences. This is a huge problem in production systems where thousands of customer messages need to be processed automatically every day. This project teaches you how to measure that inconsistency, understand why it happens, and fix it systematically using proven prompt engineering techniques. By the end, all three prompts went from being wildly inconsistent to achieving over 80% consistency — meaning the AI gave the same style of correct answer more than 8 out of every 10 times, which is reliable enough for real-world use.
